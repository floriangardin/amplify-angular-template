1. What is Data Governance?

Data governance is the coordinated set of decision rights, responsibilities, and processes that ensure the effective and efficient use of data to enable an organization to achieve its goals. It covers how data is created, described, secured, accessed, shared, and retained—across departments and systems.

Decision rights

Who can decide how data is defined, used, and changed.

Accountability

Clear ownership for quality, security, and lifecycle.

Enablement

Practical processes and tools that help teams do the right thing.

2. Why it matters now

  • AI-readiness: trustworthy data, lineage, and controls are prerequisites for safe AI and analytics.
  • Security & privacy: regulations (GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA, etc.) require consistent policy enforcement and evidence.
  • Efficiency: shared definitions and reusable assets reduce duplication and rework across teams.
  • Business agility: governed, discoverable data accelerates product and insight delivery.
Tip: start small. Pick a few high-impact domains (e.g., Customer, Product) and show measurable improvement within 90 days.

3. Core principles

Value-driven

Prioritize data products and controls that unlock clear business outcomes.

Federated, not fragmented

Empower domains with guardrails and a lightweight central backbone.

Default to open (within policy)

Maximize reuse and discovery while honoring sensitivity and consent.

Automate first

Codify policies and checks in pipelines; avoid manual gates where possible.

4. Roles & responsibilities

Data Owner

Accountable for a data domain’s fitness for purpose and policy adherence.

  • Approves definitions, classifications, and access models.
  • Prioritizes improvements and resolves conflicts.

Data Steward

Ensures quality, metadata, and issue triage for datasets and products.

  • Maintains glossary, lineage, and data quality rules.
  • Monitors KPIs and coordinates fixes with engineers.

Data Custodian (Engineering)

Implements controls in pipelines, storage, and serving systems.

  • Applies security, lineage capture, and testing in code.
  • Operates SLAs and reliability of data products.

Data Consumer

Uses governed data for analysis, ML, and products, providing feedback.

  • Follows usage guidelines and reports issues.
  • Contributes improvements and derived products.

5. Operating model

Balance central standards with domain autonomy using three layers:

Central

Policies, common tech, templates, certification, and oversight.

Domain

Ownership, quality management, access models, and roadmaps.

Product

Data contracts, SLAs/SLOs, observability, and changelog discipline.

Governance forums: monthly council (priorities, risks), bi-weekly steward sync (issues, standards), on-demand review board (exceptions).

6. Data lifecycle & controls

1) Ingest

  • Source registration, data contracts, and PII tagging.
  • Schema validation and lineage capture.

2) Transform

  • Unit tests, DQ rules (freshness, completeness, uniqueness).
  • Change management and versioning.

3) Serve

  • Access control, masking, consent enforcement.
  • SLAs, documentation, and issue channels.

7. Policies, standards, and processes

Policies

Non-negotiables: classification, retention, access, privacy, acceptable use.

Standards

Naming, documentation, schema evolution, data product quality bars.

Processes

Access requests, issue management, exception handling, deprecation.

Evidence

Audit trails, approvals, and automated control status for compliance.

8. Tooling & automation

Aim for policy-as-code and observability-by-default:

  • Catalog & lineage: searchable metadata, glossary, and end-to-end lineage.
  • Quality & testing: automated checks, expectations, and alerts in CI/CD.
  • Access & privacy: role-based access, masking, and consent-aware serving.
  • Monitoring: freshness, volume, SLIs/SLOs surfaced to owners and consumers.

9. Metrics & KPIs

Trust & quality

  • Freshness SLO adherence
  • Completeness and uniqueness scores
  • Incidents to resolution time

Adoption & value

  • Active data products and consumers
  • Reuse rate of certified assets
  • Time-to-first-insight

10. Implementation roadmap (90–180 days)

  1. Mobilize: charter, roles, domains, and success metrics.
  2. Baseline: inventory critical datasets, assess quality and risks.
  3. Pilot: ship 2–3 certified data products with policy-as-code controls.
  4. Scale: publish standards, templates, and train stewards across domains.
  5. Operate: monthly reviews, KPI dashboard, continuous improvement backlog.
Deliverables: governance charter, RACI, glossary, classification standard, data product template, DQ policy, access model, KPI board.

11. FAQ

Is governance just bureaucracy?

Good governance removes friction: reusable definitions, clear ownership, and automated controls. If it slows teams down, simplify or automate.

Centralized or federated?

Federated with strong guardrails works best: central policy and tooling, domain-level ownership, and product-level SLAs.

Where do we start?

Pick one or two high-value domains, define owners and stewards, and ship certified data products with visible quality KPIs.

12. Glossary

Data Product
A governed, discoverable, and reliable data asset serving a defined use-case with SLAs.
Lineage
Record of how data was created and transformed, helping assess impact and trust.
Classification
Sensitivity label (e.g., Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted) that guides controls.
Data Quality Rule
Automated expectation that validates correctness (e.g., freshness < 24h, nulls < 1%).
